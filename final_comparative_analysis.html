2. Mathematical Formulation¶

2.1 Problem Statement¶

We solve a multiclass logistic regression problem using softmax classification:

Objective Function: $$ \min_{\theta \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times K}} \; J(\theta) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \ell(\theta; x_i, y_i) + \frac{\lambda}{2} \|\theta\|_F^2 $$

where:

  • $\ell(\theta; x_i, y_i) = -\log P(y_i | x_i; \theta)$ is the cross-entropy loss
  • $P(y = k | x; \theta) = \frac{e^{x^T \theta_k}}{\sum_{j=1}^{K} e^{x^T \theta_j}}$ (softmax)
  • $\lambda = 10^{-4}$ is the L2 regularization coefficient
  • $d = 64$ features (8×8 images), $K = 10$ classes, $N$ samples

2.2 Gradient¶

$$ \nabla_{\theta} J(\theta) = \frac{1}{N} X^T (P - Y) + \lambda \theta $$

where $P \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times K}$ are predicted probabilities, $Y \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times K}$ is the one-hot encoded labels.

2.3 Optimization Algorithms¶

Gradient Descent (GD)¶

$$ \theta^{(t+1)} = \theta^{(t)} - \alpha \nabla J(\theta^{(t)}) $$

  • Learning rate: $\alpha = 0.1$
  • Update: Full batch gradient

Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation)¶

$$ \begin{align} m^{(t)} &= \beta_1 m^{(t-1)} + (1 - \beta_1) \nabla J(\theta^{(t)}) \\ v^{(t)} &= \beta_2 v^{(t-1)} + (1 - \beta_2) (\nabla J(\theta^{(t)}))^2 \\ \hat{m}^{(t)} &= \frac{m^{(t)}}{1 - \beta_1^t}, \quad \hat{v}^{(t)} = \frac{v^{(t)}}{1 - \beta_2^t} \\ \theta^{(t+1)} &= \theta^{(t)} - \alpha \frac{\hat{m}^{(t)}}{\sqrt{\hat{v}^{(t)}} + \epsilon} \end{align} $$

  • Hyperparameters: $\alpha = 0.001$, $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.999$, $\epsilon = 10^{-8}$

L-BFGS (Limited-memory BFGS)¶

$$ \theta^{(t+1)} = \theta^{(t)} - \alpha^{(t)} H^{(t)} \nabla J(\theta^{(t)}) $$

  • $H^{(t)}$ approximates the inverse Hessian using $m = 10$ recent gradient pairs
  • Line search: Wolfe conditions with $c_1 = 10^{-4}$, $c_2 = 0.9$
  • Convergence: $\|\nabla J(\theta)\| < 10^{-5}$

8. Analytical Discussion¶

8.1 Convergence Speed Analysis¶

What the results actually showed:

In our experiment, L-BFGS converged in just 86 iterations and finished in about 0.141 seconds. Gradient Descent and Adam both ran for 1,000 iterations and took around 0.77 seconds each. All three algorithms reached 100% validation accuracy, but L-BFGS got there in a fraction of the time and steps.

Here is why each algorithm behaved the way it did:

  1. L-BFGS (Quasi-Newton)

    • Theory: L-BFGS is a second-order method, meaning it does not just follow the slope — it also looks at the curvature of the loss surface to figure out the best step direction and size. This is like the difference between walking downhill blindly versus having a topographic map that tells you exactly where the valley is.
    • Observed: Converged in 86 iterations (~0.141s), achieving 100% validation accuracy.
    • Why it won: Because our problem is small (only 64 features, 10 classes) and convex (there is one correct minimum), L-BFGS could exploit the curvature information fully. Each step it took was much more informed than the steps GD or Adam took, so it needed far fewer of them. Think of it as taking 86 well-aimed steps versus 1,000 random ones.

  2. Adam (Adaptive Learning Rates)

    • Theory: Adam keeps track of how much each weight has been updated recently and adjusts the step size for each weight individually. Weights that have been updated a lot get a smaller step; weights that barely moved get a larger one.
    • Observed: Took 1,000 iterations and ~0.771s — similar total time to GD but with a smoother convergence curve.
    • Why it performed this way: Adam is designed primarily for noisy, large-scale problems (like deep learning). Our dataset is small and clean, so Adam's adaptive machinery did not give it a big advantage over plain GD here. It is a powerful tool, but not the best fit for this type of problem.

  3. Gradient Descent (First-Order)

    • Theory: GD simply moves in the direction of steepest descent by a fixed step size every iteration. It is the simplest and most straightforward approach.
    • Observed: Also took 1,000 iterations and ~0.772s to reach 100% accuracy.
    • Why it is the slowest in iterations: GD has no memory of past steps and no sense of curvature. It takes the same type of step every single time, even when a smarter step would converge much faster. With a learning rate of 0.1, it made steady but slow progress.

8.2 Computational Efficiency¶

The core trade-off: cost per step vs. number of steps needed

It might seem odd that L-BFGS — which does more computation per iteration — still ends up the fastest overall. Here is the intuition:

  • GD and Adam are cheap per iteration (just a gradient calculation), but they need thousands of iterations to converge. It is like taking 1,000 small, cheap steps.
  • L-BFGS is more expensive per iteration (it computes and stores gradient history to estimate curvature), but it needs only 86 steps. It is like taking 86 larger, smarter steps.

For our problem, the 86 smart steps won easily — 0.141s vs ~0.77s for the others. This gap would close or even reverse if the dataset were much larger (millions of samples), because the cost per L-BFGS step would grow too much.

In simple terms: L-BFGS is like a GPS-guided route — it takes fewer turns but each turn requires more thinking. GD is like walking without a map — you take many small steps in the roughly right direction.

8.3 What the Numbers Mean in Plain Terms¶

Looking at our actual results:

  • All three algorithms achieved 100% test accuracy — meaning for this specific dataset, all of them eventually learned to classify the 10 shapes perfectly. The difference is not whether they got there, but how fast.
  • L-BFGS was ~5.5× faster in wall-clock time compared to GD and Adam (0.141s vs ~0.77s).
  • L-BFGS used ~11.6× fewer iterations (86 vs 1,000) — meaning it needed far less "thinking" to arrive at the same answer.
  • GD and Adam were nearly identical in total time on this problem, which tells us that Adam's extra machinery (momentum tracking, adaptive rates) did not meaningfully help or hurt on this small, clean dataset.

This confirms what optimization theory predicts: second-order methods like L-BFGS are superior for small, convex, batch problems — exactly what we have here.

8.4 Practical Recommendations¶

Based on our results, here is a simple guide for choosing an optimiser:

Scenario Recommended Algorithm Why
Small dataset, batch training (like ours) L-BFGS Fastest convergence, fewest iterations, best for convex problems
Large dataset, deep learning Adam Handles noisy gradients well, works with mini-batches
Quick experiment or simple convex problem Gradient Descent Easiest to implement, predictable behavior
Non-convex neural networks Adam / AdamW Proven track record, less likely to get stuck

The key rule of thumb: if you can afford to load all your data at once and your problem is convex, use L-BFGS. If you are training a neural network on a large dataset, use Adam.

8.5 Real-World Applications¶

These three optimisers are not just academic tools — they are used in real systems every day:

L-BFGS in the real world:

  • Used in scientific computing and physics simulations where high precision and fast convergence matter more than scalability.
  • Powers many classical machine learning models in scikit-learn (e.g., Logistic Regression with solver='lbfgs').
  • Used in computer vision pipelines for calibration, 3D reconstruction, and parameter fitting.
  • Applied in natural language processing for training smaller, well-defined models like maximum entropy classifiers.

Adam in the real world:

  • The default optimiser for training neural networks — from image classifiers to language models like GPT.
  • Used to train medical imaging AI (e.g., tumor detection, MRI analysis) where datasets are large and noisy.
  • Powers recommendation systems at companies like Netflix and Spotify where models are constantly updated.
  • Used in robotics and reinforcement learning where gradient estimates are noisy and environments change.

Gradient Descent in the real world:

  • The conceptual foundation behind almost every modern optimiser — understanding GD is the first step to understanding Adam, L-BFGS, and everything else.
  • Still used in signal processing and control systems for simple parameter tuning tasks.
  • Variants like Stochastic GD (SGD) power many production deep learning pipelines, especially in cases where simplicity and predictability are valued over sophistication.

For our specific problem — shape recognition from small grayscale images:

  • A system like this could be embedded in a quality control pipeline on a factory production line, checking whether manufactured parts match expected geometric shapes.
  • It could power a simple educational app that recognises shapes drawn by students.
  • The lightweight nature of an 8×8 softmax classifier means it could run on low-power embedded hardware (microcontrollers, IoT sensors) where memory and speed are constrained.

8.6 Limitations and Future Work¶

  1. L-BFGS does not scale to large models. It needs to store gradient history in memory. For a model with millions of parameters (like a deep neural network), this becomes impractical.

    • What to do instead: Use Adam or SGD with momentum for deep learning tasks.

  2. Our dataset is small and clean. 1,200 training samples with perfect labels is a very controlled setting. Real-world data is messier.

    • Next step: Test all three optimisers on a noisier, larger dataset like CIFAR-10 or a real industrial inspection dataset to see how robustly each one handles imperfect data.

  3. We used a linear model (softmax regression). This works great for linearly separable shapes but would struggle with more complex, overlapping patterns.

    • Next step: Replace the softmax classifier with a small convolutional neural network (CNN) and compare how the optimisers perform on a non-convex problem.

  4. We only measured speed and accuracy. In a real deployment, other factors matter too — memory usage, stability across random seeds, sensitivity to hyperparameters, and energy consumption.

    • Next step: Add robustness experiments: run each optimiser 10 times with different random seeds and report the variance in convergence time and final accuracy.

9. Conclusion¶

Summary of Findings¶

This experiment compared three well-known optimisation algorithms — Gradient Descent, Adam, and L-BFGS — on a 10-class shape recognition task using 8×8 grayscale images and a softmax logistic regression model.

Here is what we found:

  1. L-BFGS was the clear winner for this type of problem:

    • Converged in just 86 iterations compared to 1,000 for both GD and Adam
    • Finished in ~0.141 seconds — about 5.5× faster than the others
    • Achieved the lowest final loss value, meaning its solution was the most mathematically precise
    • Reached 100% validation and test accuracy

  2. Adam performed solidly but showed no significant advantage over plain GD on this small, clean dataset. Its adaptive learning rate machinery is more useful when gradients are noisy or the dataset is large — neither of which applies here.

  3. Gradient Descent worked, just slowly. It is the simplest algorithm, and on a convex problem with a reasonable learning rate it will always get there — it just takes more steps than smarter methods.

Key Takeaways¶

  • Smarter steps beat more steps. L-BFGS took 86 well-informed steps; GD took 1,000 blind ones. For problems where you can afford to compute curvature, second-order methods pay off enormously.

  • The right tool depends on the problem. L-BFGS dominates on small, convex, batch problems. Adam dominates in deep learning. GD is the foundation that everything else is built on.

  • All three algorithms reached the same final accuracy. This tells us that for a simple, well-structured problem like ours, the quality of the final answer does not depend on the optimiser — only the speed of getting there does. In harder problems (non-convex, noisy), the choice of optimiser can also affect the final quality.

  • Convergence curves are informative. By plotting the loss and accuracy over iterations, we can visually diagnose whether an algorithm is struggling, oscillating, or converging cleanly — something a single accuracy number cannot tell you.

Implications for Practice¶

For anyone building machine learning systems:

  • If your model is small and your data fits in memory — reach for L-BFGS first. It will almost always be faster and more precise than GD or Adam for convex problems.
  • If you are training a neural network on a GPU with large batches — use Adam. It handles the noise and scale that L-BFGS cannot.
  • If you are learning or prototyping — start with Gradient Descent. It is transparent, easy to debug, and helps build intuition for how optimisation works before moving to more complex methods.
  • Always plot your convergence curves. A flat loss curve means your learning rate is too small. A spiking curve means it is too large. A smooth downward curve means your setup is working correctly.
